Overview

This tool generates biome transitions for hex-crawl tabletop campaigns. It is inspired by The Nerdd's D&D Hex Crawl Generator System, which uses a d10 table of five biomes (Plains, Hills, Mountains, Forest, Desert/Tundra). That system was a great starting point, but the five biomes conflate different geographic axes — terrain shape, climate, and vegetation — in ways that make some biomes hard to categorise. Desert/Tundra in particular lumps two climatically opposite environments together simply because both have sparse vegetation.

This generator expands to 14 biomes built on three clean axes: terrain (Hills, Mountains, Plateau), climate (Desert, Tundra), and vegetation (Temperate Forest, Jungle, Boreal). Water bodies large enough to occupy a full hex are treated as biomes in their own right. Rivers belong on hex edges; bays, straights, and ponds are local features, not hex biomes.

Hex Scale

These tables are calibrated for 3-mile hexes — one hex equals roughly one day of travel on foot at a cautious pace. At this scale, real-world biomes are compressed: a "forest" spanning 10 hexes covers only about 30 miles, far smaller than any real-world forest. This is intentional. A fantasy hex crawl benefits from variety and discovery, not geological accuracy. The same→same inertia values (40–55% for most biomes) produce average biome runs of 2–5 hexes, or 6–15 miles, which keeps the terrain interesting without feeling random.

Affinity Clusters

Biomes are grouped into five clusters. Transitions within a cluster are more likely than transitions across clusters. Cross-cluster transitions follow specific geographic rules — some are common (Boreal → Tundra, the treeline), some are rare but valid (Desert → Saltwater, coastal deserts), and some are near-zero (Jungle → Tundra).

Elevation biomes (Grassland, Arid Grassland, Hills, Mountains, Plateau) transition along elevation gradients. Mountains most commonly descend into Hills, which descend into Grassland. Plateau sits beside Mountains and Arid zones.
Forest biomes transition along temperature and moisture gradients. Temperate Forest ↔ Boreal is the altitude/latitude line; Temperate Forest ↔ Jungle is the tropical margin. Forest biomes rarely transition directly to Arid without an intermediate Elevation or Transitional step.
Arid biomes (Desert, Tundra) connect readily to each other and to Arid Grassland and Plateau. Desert can border Saltwater directly (Atacama, Namib). Tundra borders Freshwater frequently (glacial lakes).
Transitional biomes (Wetlands, Coast/Shore) are connectors. They have elevated cross-cluster probability in both directions. Coast sits between any land biome and Saltwater. Wetlands blur into Forest, Jungle, and Freshwater.
Water biomes have very high inertia. Saltwater only exits to Coast/Shore. Freshwater exits to Wetlands, and reflects the surrounding land biome (boreal lakes, tropical floodplains).
Rain Shadow Modifier

Mountain ranges create rain shadows: the windward side receives abundant rainfall and tends toward Forest, while the leeward side is starved of moisture and tends toward Arid Grassland or Desert.

When the modifier is active, the five Elevation cluster biome rows are rescaled:

ModeArid transitionsForest transitions
Leeward (drier)×2 (doubled)×0.5 (halved)
Windward (wetter)×0.5 (halved)×2 (doubled)

After applying multipliers the row is renormalised to sum to 100. The same→same diagonal absorbs any rounding drift. Apply the modifier only when crossing a Mountain or Highland hex in your world's prevailing wind direction, not universally — that preserves geographic coherence.

How to Roll

Roll percentile dice (d100 or two d10s read as tens/units, 00 = 100). Find your current biome's card in the Lookup Tables tab, then scan the roll ranges top to bottom until your result falls within one. That is your next hex's biome.

If you roll the same biome twice in a row, lean into it — you are deep in continuous terrain. Consider adding a local feature (a river crossing, a ruined watchtower, an unusual rock formation) to break visual monotony without changing the biome.

For hexes with two biomes, roll twice (rerolling true duplicates) and combine. Forested Hills, Desert Coast, Mountain Wetlands — the intersection is often more interesting than either alone.
